Our History & Culture

Learn about MIMAROPA's heritage

History

Pre Colonial Era

Before foreign influence, the islands of MIMAROPA were inhabited by diverse indigenous groups such as the Mangyan of Mindoro, Tagbanua and Palaw’an of Palawan, and early communities in Marinduque and Romblon. These groups practiced traditional agriculture, weaving, pottery, and maritime trade with Chinese, Malay, and other Southeast Asian traders. Their societies were organized into small settlements, each with unique customs, belief systems, and cultural traditions that remain preserved today.

Colonial Period (Spanish & American Era)

During the Spanish era, Mindoro became a mission and agricultural center; Marinduque served as a garrison and stopover; Romblon developed shipbuilding and marble industries; and Palawan played a strategic administrative and missionary role. Christianity, town-based governance, and new cultural influences spread, though many indigenous communities maintained autonomy.

Under the American period, the provinces experienced growth in education, transportation, and government systems. New roads, schools, and public institutions were built, and migration from other parts of Luzon increased, especially in Palawan and Mindoro.

Modern History & Regional Formation

After World War II, the provinces developed economically through agriculture, fishing, quarrying, and rising tourism—especially in Palawan and Mindoro.

On May 17, 2002, Executive Order No. 103 officially created MIMAROPA (Region IV-B) by separating the old Southern Tagalog region into CALABARZON (IV-A) and MIMAROPA (IV-B). A brief 2016 order to move Palawan to Western Visayas was later revoked, keeping all five provinces together. Today, MIMAROPA is known for its ecological diversity, indigenous heritage, and world-class tourism destinations such as Coron, El Nido, and Puerto Galera.

Festivals & Traditions

    Cultural & Religious Festivals

  • Moriones Festival (Marinduque)

    • One of the most colorful Lenten traditions in the Philippines, featuring masked “Morions” reenacting the story of Longinus. The whole province transforms into a stage of theatrical reenactments, processions, and religious devotion.

  • Pagdiwata Ritual (Palawan)

    • An indigenous thanksgiving ceremony of the Tagbanua people offering prayers, dances, and food to ancestral spirits for good harvests and protection.

  • Bila-Bila Festival (Occidental Mindoro)

    • Held in San Jose, it showcases giant butterfly costumes and street dances symbolizing transformation and hope.

    Historical & Heritage Celebrations

  • Biniray Festival (Romblon)

    • A maritime procession honoring Santo Niño, highlighting the province’s seafaring history and deep religious heritage.

  • Marinduque Founding Anniversary

    • A celebration of the province’s history, identity, and unity through parades, exhibitions, and cultural presentations.

  • Kawayan Festival (Puerto Princesa, Palawan)

    • An annual event highlighting bamboo craftsmanship and the indigenous traditions related to bamboo weaving and music.

    Local Customs, Traditions & Indigenous Practices

  • Cuyonon Traditions (Palawan)

    • Features balitao songs, folk dances, and storytelling that preserve the rich heritage of the Cuyonon people.

  • Mangyan Handicraft & Script Traditions (Mindoro)

    • The Mangyan communities preserve the surat Mangyan script and crafts like woven baskets and beaded accessories, showcasing their deep cultural identity.

  • Rice Harvest Rituals (Mindoro and Palawan Tribes)

    • Traditional ceremonies performed by indigenous groups—such as the Iraya, Hanunuo, and Palaw’an—to offer gratitude for bountiful harvests and to honor nature spirits.

Traditional Crafts & Foods

Traditional Crafts

  • Romblon Marble Craft (Romblon)

    • Known as the “Marble Capital of the Philippines,” Romblon produces high-quality marble sculptures, vases, tiles, and handicrafts carved by skilled artisans.

  • Mangyan Handwoven Baskets & Beads (Mindoro)

    • The Mangyan tribes create intricate handwoven baskets, beadwork, and traditional accessories using natural materials like nito, rattan, and bamboo.

  • Palawan Woodcarving & Souvenirs

    • Local artisans craft wooden masks, animal figures, and traditional instruments, demonstrating the province’s strong connection to nature and indigenous artistry.

  • Marinduque Paper Mâché Masks

    • Used during the Moriones Festival, these handcrafted masks are carefully molded and painted, representing Roman soldiers in vibrant detail.

Traditional Foods (Dishes)

  • Tamilok (Palawan)

    • A unique delicacy made from woodworms, often eaten fresh or with vinegar. It has a soft texture and is known for its briny, oyster-like flavor.

  • Adobong Manok sa Gata (Mindoro)

    • A Mindoro-style dish where chicken is cooked in coconut milk and spices, giving a rich, creamy taste with a hint of sweetness.

  • Sarsa (Marinduque)

    • A local meat dish simmered in coconut milk and spices, offering a flavorful taste often served during gatherings and celebrations.

  • Bukayo (Romblon & Marinduque)

    • Sweet shredded coconut simmered in sugar until caramelized—popular as local pasalubong.

Traditional Snacks & Specialty Products

  • Bibingkang Lalaki & Bibingkang Pinahiran (Marinduque)

    • Famous rice cakes with distinct textures—Bibingkang Lalaki is thick and heavy, while Bibingkang Pinahiran is coated with sweet coconut syrup.

  • Uraro Cookies (Marinduque & Palawan)

    • Soft arrowroot cookies made from locally grown uraro plants, often handmade by local communities.

  • Cashew Products (Palawan)

    • Palawan is known for its high-quality cashew nuts, turned into brittle, roasted snacks, and pastries.

  • Lamayo (Palawan)

    • A semi-dried marinated fish (often danggit or other local fish), known for its savory taste and enjoyed as a breakfast staple.
